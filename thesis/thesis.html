WEEK 3: February 9th - February 15th

This Past Week

Last week, I had a 1on1 meeting with Gwen. There I was able to talk through the steps of the features I had narrowed down. It was really helpful! We talked about what information I needed to build each feature and what are the most important aspects of those features. Although I tried doing that on my own, but having another person to talk to was really helpful. She suggested things that I hadn’t even thought about.

I met with my thesis advisor, Claire. Her insights are always extremely useful:

  • Data Sets: Claire believes that it’s really hard to get accurate data and that most datasets about accessible places tend to be false. She suggested that I spend a day going to locations around a specific area (potentially where I will be user testing my product) and come up with my own data. If I have about 20 to begin with, it should be enough to get a working demo.
  • Checklist: One of the most important parts of my application is the ability to filter locations based on specific needs of the user. Claire suggested that I use my main user, my mom, and create a checklist based on her needs. I can use this checklist to build my database as well as create a list of filters. It's important to note that this "checklist" will continue to evolve.
  • Users: Since my mom cannot be only user, she offered to reach out to the United Cerebral Palsy Organization of NYC. I will collect data near their offices in NYC so that the users can use that area when testing my product.
  • PhoneGap: Claire connected me to a professor at ITP who teaches how to develop iOS and Android PhoneGap mobile applications. Although I have already gone through some PhoneGap tutorials, having a resource that will help me when I get stuck is going to be really useful.
  • Waze Model: The ideal version of this app would include a Waze model of crowdsourcing. A single place cannot stay “accessible” forever – things change; like, construction, weather, places come and go, etc. So, if people can help modify the database as they travel, that will help build a better database. However, developing that feature will require a lot time and, potentially, additional resources (a.k.a. more developers). So, Claire suggested that in the first version of this app, there could be an option where the user can send me an email to update the information. Then I can continue to build the app to include the Waze model.
  • Itinerary Feature: A feature that I did not build in my pre-thesis final prototype was the “itinerary” feature, but the feedback I received was that this feature could possibly be one of the most important features of this application. Often times, there is a lot of planning involved for a person with a disability to go to a specific place. Claire suggested that to create a minimum viable product including this feature could involve the user simply saving the itinerary to their calendar (iphone, google, etc.). Once it’s saved to their calendar, they can share their itinerary with other people (by “inviting” other attendees).

Using Claire’s advice, I spoke to my mom to get an understanding of what her specific needs are when she is going to a specific place. This is what I have narrowed it down to:

  1. Ramps: Are ramps available? Is it steep? Does the ramp have railings?
  2. Doors: What type of door? Automatic? Push/pull? Sliding? Revolving? Is it heavy? Is it too hard to open for an able-bodied person? Is there a handicap accessible push button?
  3. Handicap Accessible Bathrooms: Number of stalls? Location of the stall (i.e. in the back)? Toilet height? Railings? Automatic flush?
  4. Aisles/Space: If it's a restaurant, are the aisles wide enough to maneuver a walker/wheelchair?
  5. Floor: Is it easy to walk on? Is it slippery? Is it carpeted?
  6. Handicap Parking Spots: How many are there? Are they close to the location you are trying to go to?
  7. Elevator Access: Is there elevator access? Is it easy to get to the elevator?
  8. Stairs (non-wheelchair user): Do the stairs have railings?

    What is your research question?

    How can we empower people with disabilities to go out and explore new places in NYC?

    What is the context?

    The Let’s Go application will be a digital tool that will provide accessible information in one central location that allows a person with a disability to plan out their day with detailed manageable and accessible navigation, activities and events. This information is often not easily available in one central place. Hence, this one application will enable people with disabilities to do the activities they want to do more efficiently. More importantly, this application will be created with an “accessibility-first” design mindset.
